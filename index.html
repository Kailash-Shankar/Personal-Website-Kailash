    Housing Assembly




    As a SWE intern at EDU Africa, I was tasked with partnering with the local organization Housing Assembly, who advocates for better housing conditions and regulation, to create an interactive mapping platform allowing residents in impoverished informal settlements to gain access to emergency services, contact to local government officials, and a platform to report local issues in their settlement, such as crime, water or power supply shortages, and forced home evictions. This platform also served as a way for Housing Assembly to advocate for better Housing conditions, and to show concrete evidence of all the issues that residents face in these settlements, so that they can push for change, and the housing crisis currently plauging Cape Town and all of South Africa can be brought to an end. In addition to improving my software development skills, building this software platform required me to work closely with the client throughout all stages of development to make sure the final product would be best suited for their needs, as well as requiring me to prioritize an intuitive, easy-to-use UI as the target audience for the platform had a low level of education and rudimentary tech literacy.

    Key Points:

  • Built full-stack MERN to facilitate mutual aid for 80,000+ underprivileged South African residents
  • Designed an interactive map allowing residents to locate emergency services and report location-based issues
  • Utilized React.js to build responsive front-end website UI, and Leaflet.js to implement interactive map
  • Collaborated closely with Housing Assembly to ensure platform met needs of target audience
  • Prioritized intuitive UI for users with low education and tech literacy


  • Undergraduate Research Assistant

    • Gainesville, FL

    UF Computational Linguistics Lab




    As a student researcher in the Computational Linguistics Lab, I am currently working with Dr. Zoey Liu to research how different data partitioning strategies (the way data is split into training, validation and testing datasets) improve and optimize Large Language Model generalizability, using morphologically segmented data as the testbed. Essentially this means changing the split of training, validation, and test data used in the model training process, giving the model the task of morphologically segmenting language data (separating words in a certain language into its component morphemes), and evaluating which one optimizes the model's ability to generalize, or do its assigned task on data it has never seen before. To do this research, I am using OLMo 2, an open-source LLM, training data from other similar experiments by other researchers, and the UF HiPerGator Supercomputer, in order to be able to access the computing power to train the model. This research is an expansion of a previous study conducted by Dr. Ziu in her 2024 paper, aiming to build on the findings and address the limitations of that study.

    Key Points:

  • Conduct research on improving LLM generalizability through partitioning techniques on morphologically segmented language data
  • Utilize OLMo-2, a 1 billion parameter LLM, to train and test data from 16 languages using UF HiPerGator
  • Work under Dr. Zoey Liu
